CISPA Halted In The Senante

Abstract: The Senate is currently working on its own cybersecurity legislation. The Senate's bill (or bills) will not be identical to CISPA so that bill is, in a sense, dead. The new bill by the Senant will likely have a different name, but once the Senate passes its legislation, it can be combined with CISPA, then both chambers approve the compromise bill and send it to the president.
Relevance: CISPA is controversial because it overrules all existing federal and state laws by saying: "Notwithstanding any other provision of law," companies may share information "with any other entity, including the federal government." It would not, however, require them to do so. This is relevant because it affects the privacy of every American who accesses the internet. The amount of information that can be shared will dramatically increase, which can threaten the cyber identity of all users.

Weekly Current Event (4/26)


New California Bill Would Allow Customers Access to What Companies Know About Them

Abstract: California Assembly Member Bonnie Lowenthal, a representative of the city of Los Angeles, has introduced a bill known as “The Right to Know Act of 2013″ that would compel California-based companies that store customers’ information to share that data with said customer upon request. If they do not comply, the companies can face legal consequences, including a civil suit on behalf of the subject.
Relevance: This bill is significant in terms of online privacy. Information is constantly being obtained from all online users, and many people are unaware of the extent of information that is being collected from them. From the moment a user connects through a web browser, the user’s presence on the internet is being tracked. This bill will most likely bring a greater awareness to the cyber identity of all online users while increasing the regulations companies have when collecting online information.
Source: DigitalTrends.com

With Netflix’s large user base, this move will help solidify the movement towards HTML5, help solidify the important of DRM capabilities, and help solidify the best practices of the web to make the jump to HTML5 standards.

Weekly Current Event (3/29)


World’s Top Supercomputer From ‘09 Is Now Obsolete, Will Be Dismantled

Abstract: Five years ago, an IBM-built supercomputer designed to model the decay of the US nuclear weapons arsenal was clocked at speeds no computer in the history of Earth had ever reached. At more than one quadrillion floating point operations per second (that's a million billion, or a "petaflop"), the aptly named Roadrunner was so far ahead of the competition that it earned the #1 slot on the Top 500 supercomputer list in June 2008, November 2008, and one last time in June 2009. Today, that computer has been declared obsolete and it's being taken offline.
Relevance: Even though this supercomputer was directly involved in the historical importance of the cyber world, its takedown is an important event in the over-arching technological world. This computer, which was the fastest in the world 4 years ago, is now no longer relevant compared to today’s online speeds, bandwidth needs, and data requirements. This article emphasizes how fast technology, whether it is for processing applications or supporting the infrastructure of the internet, is changing. Technology is a dynamically evolving entity, and it is easy to forget the internet’s capabilities and uses change along with it.
Source: ARStechnica.com

Weekly Current Event (3/1)


Firefox will block third-party cookies in a future version

Abstract: Firefox would allow all cookies from sites that a user actively visits, but it would block cookies from third-party sites if a user has not visited that cookie's origin site. Advertisers generally place third-party cookies and can collect data about a user across several websites with them. This is used to serve more targeted ads or refine where an advertising firm should spend its money.
Relevance: Less information will be sent between client computers and the webhost servers because of this change in the Firefox browser settings. Ultimately, this will reduce the amount of packets traveing through the online network.
